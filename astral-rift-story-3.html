Chapter Three

Night Shift

Dock Chief Maren Solace
Site 7-Choir, 14 years of service

Solace knew the tree's rhythms the way a farmer knows weather. Fourteen years of night shifts will do that. The old crews talked about it like machinery — production cycles, output schedules, metabolic rates. Solace didn't argue with them. She just knew that the tree had moods, and that tonight its mood was wrong.

The trunk was brighter than usual. Not by much. If you put an instrument on it, the reading would come back within normal range. But Solace didn't need an instrument. She'd spent more hours looking at this tree than anyone alive, and the orange was wrong. Too warm. Like a fever.

She poured a cup of tea and watched.

* * *

The pattern changed at 03:41.

She'd been half-watching, the way you half-watch something you've seen ten thousand times. The trunk pulses were regular — three seconds on, two off, the standard resting cycle. She could time her breathing to it. Sometimes did, on the long shifts.

Then the rhythm broke. The pulse held for six seconds instead of three. She set down her tea. The next pulse was eight seconds. The one after that didn't stop. The bioluminescence along the trunk shifted from the standard orange resting pattern to something she'd never seen. Not brighter. Different. A sequence where there had always been a cycle.

Not random. Not metabolic. Structured.

* * *

She called Vasik. He picked up on the second ring, which meant he was already awake, which meant he was always awake at this hour, which she already knew because she'd been calling him about the tree for years.

"Something's different."

"Different how?"

"Come look."

He didn't ask questions. That was why she called him instead of filing a report. Reports asked you to classify what you were seeing before you understood it. Vasik just looked.

* * *

He arrived in twelve minutes. Lab coat over pajamas, datapad under his arm, the same expression he always wore when something didn't fit his models. Focused and a little afraid.

They stood at the observation platform and watched. The trunk was running the new pattern on a loop. Short segments of structured light, pausing, repeating. Vasik held up his datapad and started recording.

"How long has it been doing this?"

"Eleven minutes. Started at 03:41."

"The vats?"

She checked the production board. "Normal. All cycles on schedule. Whatever it's doing, it's not affecting output."

Vasik watched the pattern cycle twice more. He zoomed in on his datapad, frame by frame. Solace watched him watch it. She knew the look. He was seeing something.

"What is it?"

* * *

"It looks like a response."

Solace waited. Vasik was not a man who said things before he was ready. If he said "it looks like," he meant "I'm 80% sure but I don't want to be."

"A response to what?"

He didn't answer. He was scrubbing through the recording, isolating the repeating element. Three cycles. A short pattern, structured, with a rhythm that wasn't biological.

"Kael."

"I heard you."

"A response to what?"

He lowered the datapad. The trunk kept pulsing. The vats kept running. Everything at Site 7 was normal except for the thing that wasn't normal, and the way Vasik was looking at it.

"I don't know. Something asked a question and the tree answered."

* * *

They watched for another forty minutes. The pattern repeated, identical each time, then stopped as suddenly as it started. The trunk returned to its resting cycle. Three on, two off. The fever color faded. Normal.

Vasik saved the recording. Solace filed a night shift report noting "anomalous trunk luminescence, duration 53 minutes, no production impact." Standard language. Nothing in the report would trigger a review.

Vasik kept his recording on a personal drive. He did not submit it to the Compact Science Division. When Solace asked why, he said he needed to compare it to something first.

"Compare it to what?"

"I don't know yet."

* * *

The next morning the tree was normal. The vats produced on schedule. Two Tempest hulls completed their growth cycle and detached. The old crews would have called it "the gasp" — the sound the hull makes when it seals. The new crews don't call it anything. Solace heard it twice and didn't think about it.

She thought about 03:41.

In fourteen years, the tree had never run a structured sequence. Metabolic variation, yes. Production adjustments, yes. Even the patterns Vasik had documented — the ones that changed when people entered the room. But those were reactions. Passive. The tree noticing things.

This was different. This was the tree saying something. Unprompted. At 3:41 in the morning, to an empty facility, in a pattern that repeated like it was waiting for confirmation.

Confirmation from what?

* * *

Solace finished her shift, clocked out, and walked to the mess hall. She ate breakfast. She didn't tell anyone what she'd seen. Not because it was secret. Because she didn't know how to describe it in a way that wouldn't sound like fourteen years of night shifts catching up with her.

The tree has moods. She's said this to people who smile politely and change the subject. She's stopped saying it. But it's true.

Last night the tree's mood was expectant.

It was waiting for something, and at 03:41, something arrived.
