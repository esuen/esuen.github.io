Watch ↔ iPhone Workout Sync — State Model

Every state the two apps can be in during a workout, who owns the workout, how they move between states, and where today's bug lives. Reflects the design we landed on.

The principle — two separate decisions.  Lifecycle (is the workout active or finished?) follows the owner — the iPhone, once it has seen the workout. Progress (which sets are logged) follows whichever device the user was actually using. Today's bug is one timestamp check trying to decide both at once.

The reconnection decision

When the watch reconnects holding a local workout, it asks two questions — never one timestamp comparison.

Who owns it?iPhone's lifecycle stateWhat the watch does
iPhone
(iPhone-started, or watch-started & since synced)		 iPhone still has the workout active Re-sync. Push the watch's offline progress up first, then mirror the iPhone. → State 2
iPhone iPhone has no active workout End the watch's copy. The owner finished it — the watch defers. → State 1  (the fix)
Watch
(watch-started, never synced)		 iPhone has no active workout (never saw this one) Push it up. The iPhone adopts the workout; ownership transfers to iPhone. → State 2
Today's code — one check: phoneTimestamp > localTimestamp Guesses. A stale watch always looks "more recent," so it keeps its dead workout and re-infects the iPhone.

Prevention layer (not shown as a state): an HKWorkoutSession keeps the watch app alive for the whole workout, so it rarely reaches States 3–5 in the first place. The reconciliation above is what makes it correct when a disconnect still happens.
