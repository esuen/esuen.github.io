Chapter Four

Line 7

Lead Engineer Daro Vek
Foundry Assembly Complex TH

The overnight shift at Complex TH was the best job Vek had ever had, and he would tell anyone who asked. Seven assembly lines running in parallel, each one a 200-meter sequence of robotic arms and welding arcs, all producing the same Hydra-class hulls they'd been producing since before anyone in the facility was born. Predictable. Reliable. The kind of work where the hardest decision was which ration to heat for dinner.

His father had worked the day shift on a similar line for thirty years. Same complex, different wing. Vek had grown up falling asleep to the distant vibration of assembly arms through the housing-block walls. The rhythm was comfort. Predictable output from predictable input. The kind of regularity that held a life together without asking you to think about it too hard.

He was eating that dinner when Line 7 stopped making Hydras.

* * *

He didn't notice immediately. The monitoring console showed seven green indicators, one per line, all nominal. Production rate, material feed, weld temperature, alignment tolerances. Every metric within spec. He finished eating. He checked the console again because that was the job, every fifteen minutes, a glance and a confirmation.

Line 7's indicator was still green. Steady state — all metrics converging on their targets, the way they had every shift for years. But the schematic preview, a small wireframe in the corner of the display showing what the line was currently building, was wrong.

Wrong like a different blueprint.

Vek's training manual had a section near the back, page 340, that he'd read once during orientation and never returned to. Section 9.7: "Anomalous Fabrication Readings." The instructions were simple. Do not attempt to diagnose. Do not interact with affected equipment. Document and report to shift supervisor. The section was four sentences long, shorter than the section on break room etiquette. He'd always assumed it was boilerplate.

He pulled up the full schematic instead.

* * *

The hull geometry was Foundry. Angular frame, modular joints, standard bolt pattern. He could see the Hydra in it. The basic structure was there, the way a skeleton is there under different skin. But the skin was wrong.

Sections of the wing assembly showed crystalline lattice structures. Mineral-bonded plating in a pattern he recognized from Lattice engineering manuals but had never seen on a Foundry line. The interior conduit routing showed organic growth patterns. Not welded, not machined. Grown. Like Choir bio-membrane threaded through a Foundry frame.

He understood assembly lines. Eight years of understanding them. A line is a feedback loop. Sensors read the workpiece, compare it to the template, adjust the actuators, read again. Closed loop. The output always converges on the template. If the template says Hydra, the line builds a Hydra. The line cannot want to build something else. It doesn't have wants. It has a reference signal, and it follows it.

He looked at the assembly floor through the observation window. Line 7's arms were moving with the same precision they always did. Reaching, welding, placing. But they were reaching for materials that shouldn't have been in the feed hoppers.

He checked the hoppers. Standard alloy stock. That's all that was loaded.

* * *

He ran the diagnostic. Everything nominal. He ran it again with a full material scan. The scan identified the crystalline sections as mineral-bonded substrate, origin: Lattice. The organic sections as bio-membrane, origin: Choir. Both materials were present in the assembly. Neither had been loaded into the feed system.

He sat with that for a moment. The control loop was intact. The sensors were reading the workpiece correctly. The actuators were responding to the template correctly. Every component in the feedback chain was functioning as designed.

The line's deadband — the threshold below which the monitoring system doesn't report — had hidden it. Small fluctuations in every metric, all within tolerance, all ignored. The reference signal had simply changed to something no one in the facility had authored.

Thirteen years ago, a feedback loop failure had killed three people two corridors from his childhood bedroom. He'd spent his entire career since then making sure the loop was intact. The loop was intact. That was the part he couldn't get past.

Then he checked the production template.

* * *

The template had changed at 03:41:07. A new instruction set, loaded into Line 7's production queue. The template identifier was a string he didn't recognize. Not a Foundry designation, not any designation format he'd seen in eight years of running these lines.

He checked the communications log. No incoming transmissions. No data uplinks. No firmware updates. No maintenance windows. Nothing had connected to Complex TH's systems at or before 03:41.

The template came from somewhere. The logs said it came from nowhere.

He pushed back from the console. The chair wheels squeaked on the floor and the sound was too loud for the room. He sat there for a while, looking at the screen without reading it, the way you look at a door that just opened by itself.

He called his supervisor. It was 04:15. His supervisor answered with the voice of a man who expected the overnight shift to handle things on its own.

"What is it, Vek."

"Line 7 changed its template."

Silence. Then: "Changed how?"

"It's building something else. Something with Lattice and Choir components. The template loaded at 03:41. I can't find where it came from."

A longer silence. "Don't touch anything. Document everything. I'll be there in twenty."

The instruction was "don't touch anything." Vek noticed that. Not "shut it down." Not "halt production." Don't touch.

* * *

He documented. Schematic captures, material scans, template logs, communications records. He photographed the assembly floor through the observation window. The arms still moving. The hull taking shape. The crystalline sections catching the welding light in colors that alloy doesn't make.

The hull's serial number was TH-7-0041. Sequential from the last Hydra. As far as Line 7 was concerned, this was the next unit in the production run. Same sequence. Different species.

His supervisor arrived at 04:33. Looked at the schematic. Looked at the floor. Looked at the communications log. Said nothing for two minutes. He didn't ask what it was.

"Who else knows?"

"Just me. Overnight shift is solo."

"Keep it that way."

* * *

By morning, the hull was impounded. A Compact team arrived that Vek wasn't introduced to. They took his documentation. They took his photographs. They asked him to describe what he saw and he described it and they wrote it down and didn't ask follow-up questions.

Line 7 resumed standard production the next day. Hydra-class hulls. Same as before. The line didn't acknowledge the deviation. No error logged. No diagnostic flag. The template change at 03:41:07 was still in the log but the template itself was gone, overwritten by the standard Hydra blueprint as if it had never been there.

Vek filed his incident report. It was accepted. He was not reprimanded. He was not debriefed further. He was told that the investigation was being handled by a different department and that his cooperation was appreciated.

Nobody told him which department.

* * *

He still works the overnight shift. He still checks the console every fifteen minutes. Lines 1 through 6 build what they've always built. Line 7 builds Hydras.

He watches Line 7 more carefully than the others now. Not because he expects it to happen again. Because he wants to understand how it happened the first time. A closed feedback loop received a new reference signal from outside the system. Materials that weren't loaded appeared in the workpiece. A hull combined three factions' technology as if the boundaries between them were a filing convention, not a physical law.

He went back and reread section 9.7 of the training manual. Four sentences. He counted the words. Thirty-one. He wondered how many times this had happened before, to produce those thirty-one words. He wondered who wrote them.

Somewhere in the Foundry's institutional history, someone had stood in a control room like this one and watched a line build something it wasn't told to build. That person had been specific enough to write a protocol and vague enough to fit it in four sentences. They'd known exactly what Vek would need to hear and exactly how little they could say.

He requested access to cross-faction engineering references from the Compact Technical Library. The request was denied. The stated reason: "Materials not relevant to current assignment."

He submitted the request again. He doesn't know why. The answer will be the same.

At 03:41 every night, he looks at the console. Line 7 is always green. He looks anyway.
