Signal
Chapter One

Corridor 31

Navigator Kes Pell
Outermark Survey Vessel Drift Margin

The corridor was empty for six hours before it wasn't.

Pell had been documenting wall composition. Mineral deposits, structural erosion, the usual. Outermark Survey paid by the kilometer and corridor 31-Omega was long, straight, and unremarkable. Good money for boring work. She liked boring.

She'd picked up the contract three months after the creditors closed her father's station account, and steady was the only variable that mattered when you'd grown up watching a family freight business decompose one bad quarter at a time.

She'd eaten half a ration bar and was considering the other half when the display changed.

The sensor scope went from twelve ambient contacts to a number the instrument couldn't render. No gradual climb. The counter flickered past four digits and switched to density mode, a setting she'd never seen it use. The scope's sampling rate was 40 kilohertz. Anything above Nyquist would alias, fold back, look like noise. What she was seeing wasn't noise. It had been hiding below the noise floor — the threshold under which her instruments treated everything as background static. Whatever was out there had been present the entire time, just quieter than the scope's ability to distinguish signal from nothing. Now it was louder than everything else. The signal-to-noise ratio had inverted. The contacts were the signal. The empty corridor behind her was the noise.

She put the ration bar down.

* * *

The scope showed the corridor ahead as solid. Not objects in space but a wall of contacts, so dense the gaps between them fell below sensor resolution. The display painted it as a single mass, amber on black, filling the forward arc from floor to ceiling.

She ran the numbers out of habit. At this range, at this density, the contact volume implied something on the order of a small moon. But distributed. Granular. No natural debris field maintained this kind of spatial uniformity. Rock clusters followed power-law distributions, clumped and sparse. This was even. Packed like a manufactured thing.

Through the viewport she could see it. Distant, still far enough that individual shapes were impossible, but the flashes were clear. Hundreds of them. Small, sharp, scattered across the dark like a storm seen from above. Something was fighting out there. A lot of somethings.

She checked the scope's diagnostic panel. Instruments nominal. Calibration current. She ran a ghost check — looking for reflections, sensor echoes bouncing off corridor walls that the scope might misread as real contacts. Ghost signals were common in tight passages. The scope returned clean. These weren't reflections. She checked the navigation log. Corridor 31-Omega, kilometer 4,200, heading inward. Nothing on the Outermark charts indicated anything at this position.

Nothing on anyone's charts indicated anything at this position.

* * *

She started the recording. Standard protocol: timestamp, position, heading, scope capture. Her hands were steady. She'd done a thousand survey logs. Her left thumbnail was black from a hatch pin three weeks ago, and she watched it move across the console with the detachment of someone who has already separated from what is happening.

The recording ran for ninety seconds. Later, the Compact would analyze every frame. They would count contacts. They would estimate density. They would classify the energy signatures. They would spend eleven years writing the same paragraph about it in their annual review.

Pell didn't count anything. She watched.

The flashes weren't random. She'd spent enough time in Rift space to know what random looked like. Debris collisions, static discharge, the slow grind of rock against rock in a dead corridor. This had coordination. Groups of flashes clustering, moving together, breaking apart. Patterns forming and dissolving faster than she could track.

And between the flashes, where the fighting wasn't happening, she could see the other thing. Harder to make out. Slower. Shapes that weren't shooting. Shapes that were building. Taking the pieces apart and putting them back together in the gaps.

Her mother had taught her that. Not in words. In the way she'd sit at the nav console for hours without speaking, reading the corridor the way other people read weather. Pell had inherited the silence and the patience. She hadn't expected to need them for this.

She thought about corridor surveys. Nine years of cataloging walls that someone else built. Nine years tracing corridors. She knew the width of every passage, the resonance signature of every junction. She'd never found what they connected to.

She'd stopped wondering about that in her third year. The Union academy had been specific: you draw what you measure. Everything beyond the measurement is someone else's problem. It had sounded like professionalism. It might have been permission to stop asking.

The scope pinged. A frequency she didn't recognize.

* * *

Not a communication signal. Not a navigational beacon. The scope logged it as environmental, background noise technically. But background noise doesn't have structure. This had structure. A short pattern, repeating. The scope captured three cycles before it stopped.

She stared at the waveform on the secondary display. The pattern's bandwidth was narrow, almost monochromatic. Clean. She'd processed thousands of waveforms in nine years and the noisy ones were always natural. Clean meant engineered. Clean meant someone had filtered out everything except what they wanted you to receive.

She saved the capture to a separate file. She did not include it in the survey log.

She didn't decide not to include it. She just didn't. Later she would try to explain this to herself and fail. It was something closer to the feeling you get when you hear your name spoken in a room where nobody knows you.

* * *

She cut thrust. The Drift Margin coasted on momentum while she plotted a return course. Three corridors between her and whatever that was. She didn't touch the throttle until all three were on the nav display, green and clear.

The trip back was quiet. The scope returned to twelve contacts. The corridor was just a corridor again. Mineral deposits. Structural erosion. The usual.

She wrote the report at her bunk that evening. Kept it short. Sensor contacts, density estimate, energy signatures consistent with autonomous combat. She recommended the Outermark Survey Office flag the area as a navigational hazard and update the charts.

She did not mention the signal.

She did not mention the signal because she had saved it to a personal file, and personal files are not part of a survey report. This is technically true. It is also the first time in nine years of survey work that Kes Pell has separated a sensor capture from its log.

The file is on her datapad. She hasn't played it back. She isn't sure why she saved it. She isn't sure why she doesn't delete it.

* * *

The Outermark forwarded her report to the Compact within a day. This was unusual. The Compact suspended her navigational license for six months, citing instrument miscalibration. This was expected. She was reinstated without explanation. This was also expected.

None of it mattered. The Bloom was there before she found it and would be there after everyone forgot her name.

But the signal is on her datapad. Three cycles of a pattern she can't read, captured at kilometer 4,200 of a corridor that the Compact now says doesn't contain anything unusual. A narrow, clean waveform. The kind of thing that only exists when someone wants it to exist.

She hasn't played it back. She will.
