Chapter Three

Taxonomy

Biologist Kess
Xenobiology, Survey Vessel Sable Reach, Outermark Expedition 9

Kess kept the samples in a quarantine locker on the lower deck, behind two sealed doors and a decontamination membrane that cost more than the rest of the ship's medical equipment combined. Standard protocol for unknown biological material. The forms alone took forty minutes.

She had fourteen samples now, scraped from the corridor walls at fifty-kilometer intervals over the last three days. Each one sealed in a spectrographic vial, each one labeled in her careful handwriting because the label printer had jammed on day two and she hadn't asked Nava to fix it yet. She liked her own handwriting better anyway. It was the one thing her graduate advisor had never criticized.

Everything else had been criticized, productively and at length. Her sample technique, her dating methodology, her tendency to reach conclusions before the data was complete. Her advisor was a meticulous woman who had studied under Kess's mother and carried forward the same insistence on precision. Kess resented the criticism while it was happening and missed it now, standing in a quarantine lab on a survey vessel fourteen hundred kilometers past the last beacon, labeling samples in handwriting no one would check.

The first seven samples were ordinary. Mineral crust, trace organics, nothing that wouldn't appear in any Outermark corridor survey. She'd catalogued identical material during her graduate work and could have written the analysis in her sleep.

Sample eight was different.

* * *

The growth was pale green under the work lamp. Filamentous, spreading across the wall in a branching pattern that reminded her of mycelium. She'd found it growing in a shallow groove on the corridor surface, thriving in the gap between two wall panels where moisture had no business existing.

Under magnification, the cellular structure was wrong.

Kess had studied Choir biotech for six years. The Choir grew their ships, their tools, their architecture from engineered organisms. It was the foundation of their civilization, the thing that made them Choir instead of anything else. She knew their cellular patterns the way a musician knows scales. The branching geometry. The nutrient exchange membranes. The way their organisms folded proteins along stress lines to create structural elements from living tissue.

This sample had all of that. The same branching ratios. The same membrane structures. The same protein folding patterns.

She ran a dating analysis. Then she ran it again, recalibrating the isotope reference against the most conservative baseline she had. Then she sat very still for several minutes, her hands flat on the worktable the way they always went when she was thinking hard.

Her mother had once described the feeling of a result that changes the question. "You run the analysis expecting one of two outcomes. You get a third. And the third is the one that means your framework was wrong." Kess had thought it was a theoretical observation. It was a warning.

The sample predated the Choir's earliest archaeological record by approximately nine thousand years. It wasn't that the Choir had been here first. The Choir hadn't existed yet.

* * *

She told herself it was contamination. Some Choir vessel had passed through here centuries ago, left biological residue, and the dating method was wrong. Dating methods were wrong all the time with Rift materials. The environment played tricks with isotope ratios. Everyone knew that.

Then she opened sample eleven.

Crystalline. A lattice of interlocking mineral fibers growing from the wall surface in a tight geometric pattern. She recognized the bonding structure immediately. Lattice mineral technology. The same crystalline matrix that Lattice engineers used to build everything from hull plating to communication arrays. The molecular geometry was unmistakable.

She dated it. Older than sample eight. Older than the Lattice civilization by a similar margin.

Sample twelve was angular. Hard panels of composite material growing from the wall in overlapping plates, each one precisely angled. Foundry manufacturing patterns. The same material science, the same structural philosophy. Kess could have pulled a Foundry hull sample from her reference library and set it next to this growth, and any student would have classified them as related.

She dated it. Same timeframe as the others.

Three samples. Three faction technologies. All older than the factions themselves. Kess pulled up a blank display and started drawing a tree, the way she'd been trained. Organism at the tip of each branch. Shared ancestor at each fork. You built the tree by tracing backward from the present, looking for where the lines converged.

She drew the Choir biotech at one branch tip. Lattice crystal at another. Foundry composite at the third. Standard procedure: three independent technologies that arrived at similar solutions through different paths would produce a tree with three branches diverging from separate roots. Parallel development. Independent invention. The comfortable explanation.

But every line she traced backward led to the same place.

* * *

Sample thirteen was the one she couldn't classify at all.

Filaments. Hair-thin threads of an unknown material woven through the wall substrate in patterns that looked almost like text. They weren't organic. They weren't mineral. They weren't any composite she had in her reference database. Under the spectrometer, the material returned readings she'd never seen before. Under magnification, the weave pattern shifted depending on the angle of observation, the way certain metamaterials changed properties when you rotated them.

She photographed it from twelve angles. The pattern was different in each image.

Sample fourteen was the one that brought it together.

She'd taken it from a junction point where two corridors met. The wall at the junction showed all four types growing together. Organic filaments interlocked with crystalline matrices, threaded through angular composite plates, all of it connected by the unknown filament material. Not layered. Not competing for space the way organisms from different origins would. Integrated. Growing as a single system with four distinct expression types.

She'd seen something like this pattern once before, in a seminar on lateral transfer in microbial communities. Organisms swapping genetic material directly, skipping the generational inheritance that evolution usually required. The result was a population where traits jumped between lineages like rumors through a crowd. It was messy, unpredictable, and it made traditional classification nearly impossible.

This was the opposite. This was clean. The design language hadn't jumped between lineages chaotically. It had been distributed from a single source, deliberately, into separate expressions. Not lateral transfer. Lateral deployment.

Kess stared at the sample under the scope for a long time. The resonance was audible here on the lower deck, a low hum that rose and fell in a cycle she could time with her breathing. Under magnification, the organic filaments in the sample pulsed in the same rhythm. Growing, contracting, growing. Imperceptibly slow. But synchronized.

The resonance frequency matched the growth cycle.

* * *

She organized her notes that evening. The tree she'd drawn on the display kept pulling her back. She'd tried to make it branch. She'd tried every configuration she knew. Root it at different nodes. Weight the morphological similarities differently. Treat the shared molecular patterns as coincidence, as contamination, as measurement artifact.

Every version of the tree collapsed into a trunk with no branches. Three technologies that should have diverged from separate origins instead converged on a single root. Not similar solutions. Identical implementations. Down to the protein folding sequences, the crystal lattice angles, the composite layering ratios. The kind of identity that didn't happen by chance, and didn't happen through parallel development, and didn't happen through any mechanism she'd been taught.

There was a simpler explanation. One that didn't require coincidence.

The factions had not developed their technologies independently and arrived at the same designs by accident. They had found these walls, or walls like them, at some point in their early history. Found the organic growths, the crystal formations, the angular composites. Studied them. Copied them. Built civilizations on what they'd learned. Then forgot where the knowledge came from, the way children forget they learned language from someone else.

Kess was twenty-six. This was her first deep-space posting, her first expedition past the Outermark boundary. She'd celebrated the assignment with her cohort at the university bar, all of them envious, all of them certain she'd come back with nothing interesting because that's what Outermark surveys always produced. She'd expected the same. She'd brought three novels and a knitting project.

She hadn't touched either.

The novels were her mother's favorites, packed as a private joke: three xenobiology thrillers where the biologist saves the station. The knitting was her own — a half-finished scarf she'd been working on for two years, the kind of project you carry because the repetitive motion calms the hands when the mind won't settle. The hands hadn't been calm since sample eight.

* * *

Kess filed the sample data with full spectral profiles and dating results. She attached no hypothesis. The data was the data. Anyone with the training to read it would reach the same conclusion. Anyone without the training wouldn't be reading xenobiology reports.

She kept one observation out of the official record. The four material types in sample fourteen were growing as a unified system, but there was something else in the junction wall she hadn't been able to sample. Deeper than her tools could reach. The spectrometer picked up trace readings of it through the surface material, the way you might hear music through a closed door.

A fifth material type. The substrate the other four grew from. The trunk of the tree that wouldn't branch.

She didn't know what it was. Her instruments weren't built to analyze it. But it was there, underneath everything, and the four types she could identify were all expressions of it. Not branches growing in different directions. More like the same organism wearing different faces for different purposes.

Kess cleaned her tools, sealed the quarantine locker, and went to the galley for tea. The hum followed her through the ship. She'd stopped noticing when it started. It had become part of the Sable Reach, the way engine noise became part of any ship you lived on long enough.

She sat in the galley with her hands around the warm cup and thought about what she'd tell her cohort when she got back. About a tree that wouldn't branch. About five civilizations that thought they'd each invented fire, all standing around the same match they'd found on the ground. She rehearsed the explanation in her head and realized she didn't understand the implications yet. Not fully. She was twenty-six and she'd trained to classify organisms, not to discover that classification itself was the wrong framework.

She wondered if the Choir had heard the hum too, once. Before they forgot.
