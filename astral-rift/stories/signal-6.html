Chapter Six

The Bloom

In the center of the field, where the units are newest and the wreckage is thinnest, a single machine stops fighting.

It is smaller than the others. Newer. Its armor is denser, its weapons more efficient, its maneuvering faster. It is, by every metric the Compact uses to evaluate combat effectiveness, the best unit in the field. The product of centuries of selection pressure, millions of iterations, a closed loop that has been refining its output since before anyone alive was born.

The loop has no designer. It had one, once, presumably. The way wolves had a common ancestor with lapdogs, somewhere back before the breeding started. What remains is the process. Combat selects for fitness. The unfit become raw material. The fit reproduce. Each generation carries forward the traits that survived and loses the ones that didn't, and the losses accumulate across centuries until the original design is unrecognizable from the current one. Except there is no breeder. No hand selecting for docility or size or color. The selection pressure is death, and it has been running unsupervised for longer than any faction has kept records.

The unit holds position. The others continue fighting. This one waits.

* * *

Its sensors reorient. Away from the field. Away from the combat. Outward, along the corridor axis, toward the navigable corridors where ships fly and stations orbit and people live inside a system they didn't build.

It holds for four seconds.

In those four seconds, the Compact's long-range monitoring station registers a minor anomaly in the Bloom's behavioral data. A single unit, center-field, stationary. The anomaly is logged automatically. It does not trigger a review. Stationary units are common. Manufacturing pauses, damage assessment, material processing. The system classifies it as routine.

Routine would have moved.

* * *

The unit resumes combat. It destroys two adjacent units in the time it takes the monitoring station to write the log entry. The wreckage drifts toward the manufacturing zone. New units will be built from it by morning. The materials will be sorted, recombined, and assembled into something slightly different from what was destroyed. Drift. Generational drift. The same way a copied sentence changes across a thousand retellings, each version faithful to the one before it and unrecognizable from the first.

The field expands. The loop continues. Build, fight, recycle, repeat. The same process that has been running since before the Compact existed. The same process that three analysts noticed matched their own simulation program. The same process that nobody has proposed a response to, because responding would require first agreeing on what it is. A weapon system. An ecosystem. A garden gone to seed. The Compact's terminology has shifted four times in five decades, each generation of analysts inheriting the previous generation's framework and slowly discovering it doesn't fit.

* * *

But for four seconds, something in the center of the Bloom looked outward. Toward the corridors. Toward the stations. Toward Room 412, where an archivist just turned her lamp back on. Toward Site 7, where a tree is quiet again. Toward Complex TH, where an engineer watches Line 7 at 03:41 every night. Toward a facility where a pilot remembers a hum she can't explain. Toward an Outermark vessel where a navigator keeps a file she won't delete.

It looked at all of them. Or past them. Or through them, at the system underneath. The corridors and the caches and the clock, the infrastructure that was old before any of them were born.

Then it went back to work.

* * *

The monitoring station's daily report contains 4,200 entries. The stationary unit is entry 3,847. It occupies one line. No flag. No annotation. No follow-up recommended.

The report is filed in the Compact's Threat Assessment Division. It will not be cross-referenced with any other department's records.

Not yet.
