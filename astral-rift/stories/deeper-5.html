Chapter Five

The Format

Signals Officer Mira
Communications, Survey Vessel Sable Reach, Outermark Expedition 9

Mira had been recording the Rift's emissions since they passed Beacon 71-F. It was her job, technically, though nobody had expected there to be anything worth recording. Outermark survey vessels carried signals equipment because regulations said they had to. Most signals officers on deep expeditions spent their time reading.

Mira had been reading for the first five days. Then the resonance showed up in her recordings, and she stopped reading. Not because of what the instruments showed. Because of what she saw.

She'd had the condition since childhood. Sound arrived as color. Low frequencies were dark amber, warm, pooling at the bottom of her visual field. High frequencies were sharp blue, bright, scattered like ice. Most people who heard sound just heard it. Mira heard it and saw it, layered over whatever she was looking at, like a transparency laid on the world. It had made her miserable as a child and exceptional as a signals officer. She could spot structure in noise the way a colorblind person couldn't spot the number hidden in the dots. Her eyes found patterns her ears alone would miss.

The station she'd grown up on had twelve docking bays and a public address system that ran eighteen hours a day. Every announcement was a wash of color — schedule changes in orange, emergency alerts in white, the daily cargo manifest in a shade of green she still associated with boredom. She'd spent the first ten years of her life learning to see through the noise. The signals academy had been quieter. The Sable Reach was quieter still. She'd chosen deep-space assignments because space itself was the only place that was actually silent.

The resonance was amber. Deep, steady, the color of old wood. But underneath it, riding on the low-frequency hum, she saw threads of something else. Pale silver. Structured. Moving.

Twelve days of continuous emissions data. She'd filled two storage modules and started a third. The equipment was designed for brief signal intercepts during Compact patrols, not for sustained recording of a broadband source. She'd had to write a compression algorithm on day eight to keep from running out of space.

The algorithm was good. Better than good — it preserved the full spectral envelope while reducing file size by a factor of twelve. She'd written it in four hours, tested it against known signal sources, and deployed it without telling anyone because it wasn't interesting enough to mention. She wrote tools the way other people wrote notes: quickly, for immediate use, and without the expectation that anyone else would read them.

* * *

The resonance was the obvious feature. Low frequency, steady amplitude, slowly increasing since they'd entered the maintained section of corridor. Everyone on the ship could hear it now. Nava said it matched the conduit network's operating frequency. Kess said it matched biological growth cycles in the wall samples. Shan said it synchronized with the patrol units at the junction.

Mira believed all of them. She also thought they were all looking at the wrong part of the signal.

The resonance was a carrier wave. The silver threads she saw underneath it were structure. She'd almost missed it on the instruments. The signal-to-noise ratio was terrible at that frequency range, and her equipment wasn't optimized for it. But she could see it, the pale filaments of information woven through the amber carrier, and once she found them in the data she couldn't stop finding them.

She ran a frequency decomposition on a ten-minute sample. Broke the signal into its component waves the way a prism breaks white light into colors. The carrier wave was one component, dominant, obvious. But underneath it were dozens of others, each at a different frequency, each carrying its own modulation pattern. They layered on top of each other cleanly, without interference, the way a chord stacks notes without canceling each other out.

She stared at the decomposition and saw it in two ways at once. On the display, a stack of waveforms. In her vision, a braid of colors running from amber to silver to something she didn't have a name for. The components weren't random. They were structured the way language is structured. Repetition, variation, hierarchy.

* * *

She pulled Drel's cartographic data and laid it alongside her signal recordings, aligned by timestamp. Then she pulled the Sable Reach's position logs and overlaid those too. Three datasets, time-synchronized.

The structure in the carrier wave changed when the corridor changed.

When they passed through a straight section, the modulation pattern was regular, repeating. The corridor was ducting the signal — focusing it the way a waveguide focuses microwave energy, the walls acting as reflective boundaries that kept the carrier wave coherent over distances that should have attenuated it to nothing. When the corridor curved, the pattern shifted. The tighter the curve, the greater the shift. At junctions, the pattern became dense with variation, then simplified again as they moved into the next straight section.

She charted the correlation over two days. She measured the information density at each point, counting the number of distinct states per unit of signal. Straight corridors were low-density: simple, repeating, the way a single sustained note carries less information than a melody. Junctions were dense, packed with variation, every frequency component shifting simultaneously. She plotted the density against the corridor dimensions and found a clean relationship. The wider the corridor, the more information it carried. The more branches at a junction, the more complex the encoding.

Then she reversed it.

She took the signal data and, using the correlation she'd mapped, predicted what the corridor ahead should look like based on the signal she was receiving now. She handed the prediction to Shan without explanation and asked him to compare it to what the forward sensors were showing.

It matched. Three junctions, two curves, and a long straight. Her prediction was accurate to within four percent.

Shan asked her how she'd done it.

* * *

"The corridor is the signal," she told the crew at the next briefing. She'd set up a display showing the correlation data, the prediction results, and a visualization of the modulated carrier wave. The colors she saw overlaying the waveform were hers alone. To the others it was just lines on a screen. Voss sat at the head of the table. The others arranged themselves around the small conference space the way they always did: Nava closest to the door, Kess with her sample case, Shan leaning against the far wall, Drel already taking notes.

"Explain," Voss said.

"The resonance is a carrier wave. It's carrying information. The information it's carrying is the physical structure of the corridor itself. Dimensions, curvature, junction topology. The geometry of the Rift is encoded in its own emissions."

"A map," Drel said.

"More than a map. A map is a representation of a thing. This is the thing describing itself. The corridor's shape is the message. The width of a passage, the angle of a turn, the number of branches at a junction. These aren't engineering constraints. They're syntax."

Silence.

"And the encoding has redundancy," Mira continued. "The same dimensional information is repeated in multiple frequency components. Offset, staggered, so that if one component is damaged or degraded, the others still carry the full content. In degraded corridors, you'd get multipath — the signal bouncing off rough walls, arriving at slightly different times, interfering with itself. The redundancy compensates. The same way you'd build fault tolerance into a data storage system. Repeat the message so that errors can be detected and corrected."

She saw it in her mind as the braid of colors, each strand carrying the same information in a different hue, so that losing one strand didn't lose the pattern.

"The Rift network isn't infrastructure that happens to carry energy," Mira said. "The energy is there to maintain the structure. And the structure is there to encode the signal. The corridors are the content."

* * *

Nava spoke first. "What does it say?"

"I don't know. I can read the format. I can't read the language. It's like intercepting a transmission in a protocol you've never seen. You can tell it's structured. You can count the packets. You can identify headers and frame boundaries. You can't decode the payload."

"How much text?" Kess asked.

Mira had done this calculation already. She'd hoped someone would ask so she wouldn't have to volunteer it.

"The Compact has mapped approximately eleven thousand kilometers of Rift corridor. Based on the encoding density in this section, that represents a small amount of data. A paragraph, maybe. The Rift network is estimated to extend over two million kilometers."

She let them do the math.

"And the corridors can't be simplified," she added quietly. "I tried. I tried to find a shorter description of the structure. A formula, a compression, any way to represent the same information in less space. There isn't one. The corridor layout is already the shortest possible description of whatever it encodes. Every dimension, every angle, every junction is load-bearing. Remove any element and the message degrades. It's not just structured. It's minimal. Someone designed this to say exactly what it says using exactly the space it takes and no more."

* * *

She spent the rest of the evening refining her analysis. The encoding was elegant once you knew what to look for. Nested structure, hierarchical organization, something that functioned like punctuation between major sections. The signal wasn't random data. It had grammar.

The carrier wave had been getting stronger since they entered the maintained section. Out in Compact space, where the walls were degraded and the conduit network was dead, the signal would be too faint to detect. Maybe absent entirely. Corrupted by centuries of decay and overuse, the redundant components lost one by one until the error correction couldn't compensate anymore. Like reading a water-damaged manuscript. The data was still there, underneath the noise. But no one could recover it.

Here, the manuscript was intact.

Mira saved her work and sat in the quiet of the signals station. The carrier wave hummed through the hull. She'd been annoyed by it for the first few days. Then she'd gotten used to it. Now she listened to it differently. The amber glow in her peripheral vision had become familiar, almost warm. She let herself watch the silver threads moving through it, carrying their patient information to no one in particular.

She'd spent her career intercepting communications between ships, stations, and governments. Messages sent by known entities with known intentions through known protocols. This was the first signal she'd encountered that wasn't addressed to anyone. It was simply present, embedded in the physical world, transmitting continuously to whatever happened to be nearby.

Mira listened. The corridors spoke. She couldn't understand the words yet. But she could see them, silver on amber, old and luminous.

Her academy advisor would have called it "structured." She wouldn't.
