Chapter Two

The Seat

Test Pilot 3
Compact Evaluation Division

The briefing was short. Sit in the seat. Don't touch anything. Report what you experience. Sixty seconds. The timer starts when you sit down.

Test Pilot 3 — they used numbers, not names, for this program — nodded and walked through the inspection bay door. She'd read the file. One ship, found drifting, no crew, no explanation. The hull that returned no readings. The cloaking event. She'd read all of it and none of it prepared her for the room.

It was wrong the way silence is wrong when you expect sound.

* * *

The cockpit was smaller than she expected. The reports said "featureless" and she'd imagined empty — a big blank space. It wasn't. The room was close. The walls curved into the floor and ceiling with no seam, no corner, no edge where one surface became another. Like being inside a closed hand.

The seat was in the center. It looked human-compatible. This was, according to the file, "the single most discussed detail." She understood why. A chair built for a body shaped like hers, inside a ship built by something that left no trace of what it looked like.

She sat down. The timer started.

* * *

The first ten seconds: nothing. The seat was comfortable. Not ergonomic — comfortable the way a shape is comfortable when it already knows what shape you are. She adjusted her weight and the seat didn't adjust with her. It didn't need to. It was already right.

The violet light had no source. She'd been told this. Seeing it was different from being told. The light wasn't coming from the walls. It wasn't ambient. It was just present the way air is present. She couldn't identify its direction because it didn't have one.

Second fifteen. The hum started.

* * *

Not through her ears. She was sure of that immediately and spent the next ten seconds trying to be less sure. She pressed her palms flat on the armrests. No vibration. She held her breath. Still there. She covered her ears. Louder, if anything.

It wasn't sound. It was in her head the way a memory of a sound is in your head — present, clear, but produced by nothing external. Except a memory fades when you focus on it. This didn't. It got more detailed.

A low tone. Steady. And under the tone, a pattern.

She stopped trying to identify it and started listening.

* * *

The pattern was short. Three cycles, repeating. It had a structure she couldn't name — not musical, not mathematical, not linguistic. Something adjacent to all three. Like a sentence in a language where the grammar is spatial instead of sequential.

She thought: I've heard this before.

She hadn't. She was certain she hadn't. But the thought arrived fully formed, like the hum itself — not generated by her, just present. The pattern felt familiar the way a face you've never seen can feel familiar. Recognition without source.

Second forty-five. She realized she wasn't counting anymore. The timer on her wrist was counting. She had stopped caring about the timer on her wrist.

* * *

Something shifted. Not the room. Not the light. Something in the pattern. A fourth element, brief, that she felt more than heard — a directional component, as if the pattern were pointing somewhere. Not at her. Past her. Through the hull, through the inspection bay, through the facility, toward something far away.

For a fraction of a second she felt what it was pointing at. Not saw. Not understood. Felt, the way you feel the size of a dark room before your eyes adjust. Something vast. Something occupied. Something that was doing the same thing she was doing right now.

Listening.

* * *

"Time."

The voice came from outside the cockpit. The technician, reading the clock. Sixty seconds. Session complete. Please exit the vehicle.

She sat for two more seconds. The hum was still there. The pattern was still repeating. The directional component was gone — or she'd lost the thread. She wasn't sure which.

She stood up. The hum stopped. Not faded. Stopped, the way a conversation stops when you leave the room.

She walked out of the cockpit, through the inspection bay, past the technician, to the debriefing room. She sat down at the table and picked up the form.

The form asked: "Describe your experience."

She wrote: "Persistent low-frequency hum. No identified source. Duration: continuous from approximately T+15 to session end."

* * *

She did not write about the pattern. She did not write about the recognition. She did not write about the directional component or the thing at the other end of it.

She didn't decide not to write it. The form asked her to describe her experience and she wrote what she experienced. The hum was an experience. The rest was something else. She didn't have a word for what it was. The form didn't have a field for it.

That evening she submitted a request to repeat the session. The request was filed at 19:42. The timestamp mattered because later, when the program was reviewed, someone would note that 19:42 was less than three hours after her first session. The average gap between session and repeat request for the other six test pilots was four days.

She filed six more requests over the following weeks. All denied.

She is currently assigned to active combat operations, flying a Specter-class vessel. The reproduction. The hull that scans differently from the original. The cloaking system that hides instead of removes.

She says the hum is there. The engineers say it shouldn't be. She says she's grown to like it.

* * *

At 03:41 on the morning after her session, she woke from a dream she couldn't remember. She reached for her datapad and typed a single line before falling back asleep.

The line read: "Something is counting."

She deleted it the next morning. She doesn't remember typing it.
