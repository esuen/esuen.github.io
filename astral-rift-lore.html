THE ARCHIVES

Recovered fragments from before and after the Collapse. Classification level: Compact General.

Chapter I

The Rifts

They appear as fractures in the geometry of space itself — jagged tears where dimensional boundaries thin to nothing. Sensors register them as gravitational anomalies. Navigation systems refuse to plot courses through them. Pilots who fly near them report hearing patterns in the static.

Before the Collapse, the Rifts were infrastructure. Transit corridors. Supply lines. Weapons testing ranges. Five civilizations built empires around them, fought wars inside them, and depended on them so completely that when the Rifts changed, everything built around them collapsed too.

The Rifts didn't close. They didn't move. They just — shifted. Whatever property made them useful became something else. The corridors still exist. The rules inside them are different now.

Archival image: Rift corridor 7-Sigma, first survey expedition, 43 years post-Collapse

Pre-Collapse scientists called it "resonance binding." They documented it thoroughly. They never explained it. After the Collapse, the documentation survived. The understanding didn't.

Compact Science Division, introductory briefing, page 1 of 1

The corridors didn't change. We did.

Chapter II

The Five

Five civilizations. Five approaches to the same problem: how to survive in Rift space, where physics is negotiable and energy is the only currency that matters. Each developed technology so distinct that modern engineers can identify the origin of a hull fragment at a glance. Four left recoverable schematics. One did not.

The Foundry
Industrial · Military · Precision

They built things the way engineers build things: with specifications, tolerances, and serial numbers. Foundry ships are angular, modular, and maintainable. Every hull plate can be replaced. Every weapon system has a technical manual. The military doctrine was simple — more ships, faster, with standardized parts.

The Foundry produced more ships than the other four factions combined. Most of them still fly. The Viper interceptor is the most common frame in modern Rift space, not because it's the best, but because there are thousands of them, and the schematics are so well-documented that any mechanic with a wrench can keep one running.

Reconstructed image: Foundry Assembly Complex, designation unknown
The Lattice
Crystalline · Mineral-Bonded · Ancient

The Lattice didn't build ships. They grew them — from mineral substrate, in crystal caverns, using processes that modern engineers can replicate but not explain. The hull plating bonds at a molecular level. The energy conduits follow natural crystal veins. The result is heavy, dense, and nearly indestructible.

Lattice technology is the oldest recoverable tech in Rift space. Carbon dating on Titan hull fragments returns dates that predate every other faction by centuries. Either the Lattice existed long before the others, or their mineral-bonding process produces anomalous isotope signatures. Both explanations are uncomfortable.

Scan reconstruction: Lattice mineral formation site, deep structure survey 12-K
The Choir
Organic · Bioluminescent · Grown

Choir ships are alive. Not metaphorically — the hull is organic tissue, the weapons are biological processes, and the munitions are grown in vats rather than manufactured on assembly lines. The bombs bounce because the casing is elastic membrane. The mines sense proximity because they have rudimentary nervous systems.

Nobody knows what the Choir actually looked like. The bio-vats still function, still producing hull material and munitions on schedules set before the Collapse. But the organisms they produce follow templates — genetic programs with no programmer. The ships grow themselves. The Choir just wrote the instructions.

Bio-survey image: Active Choir production vat, site designation classified

Four sets of schematics. Four philosophies of war. And one set of questions nobody wants to ask about the fifth.

The Fourth
Null · Unknown · Unknowable

The Fourth left no schematics. No documentation. No infrastructure. The only evidence they existed is a single ship class — the Specter — found drifting in deep Rift space with no crew, no damage, and no explanation. The hull has no seams, no welds, no fasteners. Spectroscopic analysis returns nothing. Not unknown materials — literally no reading, as if the hull isn't reflecting the beam.

The cloaking system doesn't hide the ship. It makes the ship absent — sensors don't detect it because, for the duration of the cloak, the ship genuinely isn't there. Where it goes is a question the Compact Science Division has formally declined to investigate.

Sensor artifact: Object 4-Null, recovered from deep Rift corridor. Image may not be accurate.
The Weave
Filament · Dimensional · Absent

The Weave built with dimensional filament — structures that were half matter and half geometry, stretched between anchor points in Rift space like threads in a web. Their ships weren't solid objects so much as patterns held in tension, frameworks of energy and intent that moved through Rift corridors the way a spider moves through silk.

Their schematics resist reconstruction. The filament decays outside Rift space. Every attempt to stabilize it in a laboratory has failed. But in the deep corridors, pilots still find anchor points — perfectly preserved mounting brackets for technology that no longer exists, arranged in patterns that look uncomfortably like webs. Whatever the Weave built, the architecture remains. The threads are gone.

Deep survey image: Filament anchor array, Rift corridor 19-Theta. Image enhanced; original was nearly invisible.

Five empires. One generation. No bodies. No wreckage. No war damage. Just silence.

Chapter III

The Collapse

Within a single generation, all five civilizations disappeared. Not destroyed — gone. The infrastructure survived intact. Ships drifted with open cockpits and warm engines. Power systems went dark one by one as fuel reserves depleted, not because anyone shut them down.

There was no war. No plague. No environmental catastrophe. The archaeological record shows normal activity — trade routes, construction projects, military deployments — right up until the moment it stops. Mid-sentence communications. Meals half-eaten. Experiments mid-observation with data still recording to empty rooms.

The Compact's official position is that the cause of the Collapse is unknown and not currently under investigation. This is the only topic on which the Compact and the Outermark agree without qualification.

Survey image: Station designation unknown. Power systems active. No life signs. Status: normal.

Meridian Archivist Sola Denn documented 847 pilot superstitions across four factions. 23% matched sealed pre-Collapse navigational protocols. A further 6% matched nothing in any known archive — but appeared simultaneously in all four factions, word for word.

The warnings survived. Nobody knows how.

The automated supply caches are still manufacturing equipment on schedules set centuries ago. Nobody has found the off switch. Nobody is looking.

Chapter IV

The Aftermath

Centuries later, the Rifts remain. The supply caches remain. And independent pilots have built a new civilization in the ruins of the old one — scavenging schematics, rebuilding ships from recovered blueprints, and fighting over the resources that the Collapse left behind.

Two political structures have emerged. The Compact controls the known Rift corridors, maintains the archives, and regulates access to pre-Collapse technology. The Outermark operates in uncharted space, salvaging what the Compact hasn't catalogued and selling it to whoever can pay. The relationship between them is technically diplomatic and practically adversarial.

Pilots align with one or neither. Most are independents — mercenaries, salvagers, arena fighters — flying ships built from the schematics of civilizations they know nothing about, using technology that works for reasons nobody fully understands.

Chapter V

The Bloom

In the deep zones — Rift corridors that the Compact has classified as non-navigable — veteran navigators report a region they call the Bloom. Sensor data shows an expanding field of machine wreckage: millions of autonomous combat units, still manufacturing, still fighting each other, still growing.

Whatever civilization deployed autonomous weapons in Rift space, the weapons are still there. They have been at war with themselves for longer than anyone has been alive. The wreckage field is expanding at a measurable rate. The Compact monitors it. The Outermark pretends it doesn't exist. Neither has proposed a response.

Three MTAD analysts have filed formal objections to the Compact's neural combat simulation program, citing similarities between the training methodology and the behavioral patterns observed in the Bloom. All three objections were classified. The simulations continue.

Chapter VI

The Rift Paradox

Near a Rift, physics simplifies. A single energy field powers everything — shields, weapons, propulsion, life support. The same resource that keeps you alive is the resource you spend to kill someone else. Fire a volley and your defenses drop. Take a hit and you can't shoot back.

The more dangerous you become, the more vulnerable you are.

Pre-Collapse civilizations understood this. Their ship designs reflect it — every weapon system, every defensive ability, every tactical option draws from the same pool. The Foundry solved it with efficiency. The Lattice solved it with armor. The Choir solved it with regeneration. The Fourth solved it with absence. The Weave solved it with traps.

Modern pilots solve it with skill, timing, and the willingness to risk everything on a single trigger pull.
